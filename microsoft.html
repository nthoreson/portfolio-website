Second Role

November 2021 - October 2022

Senior Communications & Project Manager

I owned internal communications for Microsoft's Service Center, the enterprise support hub for Fortune 500 clients. The job wasn't communication. It was operational redesign. Requests were stacking up, employees were spread across roles they hadn't been trained for, and a merger was changing the org chart faster than people could keep up. My work was to rebuild how requests were triaged, how employees were trained, and how the organization stayed aligned through the change.

What the role actually involved
  • Implemented enterprise communication systems that kept cross-functional teams aligned across the Service Center organization.
  • Designed and deployed training programs for 200+ employees during a restructuring and merger.
  • Reengineered the client request workflow from the ground up, cutting average processing time by 64% (from 90 days to 16).
  • Ran internal communications strategy to keep teams informed and moving through a period of significant structural change.
The 64% reduction in processing time didn't come from working faster. It came from finding where work was getting stuck and rebuilding the workflow around that constraint. The constraint was upstream of the team, not inside it.

Takeaways

What Fortune 100 Scale Taught Me

Process Is Strategy

At Microsoft, the gap between a good idea and a real result was almost always a broken process. I stopped thinking about strategy as a plan and started thinking about it as a system. The 64% improvement in request processing time wasn't a creative breakthrough. It was constraint identification and workflow redesign. That's the real work, and it's the part most teams skip because it's less interesting than the launch.

Rollouts Die Without Buy-In

You can design a clean system and watch it fail because the people executing it weren't part of building it. Training 200+ employees through a merger made that unavoidable. Communication and change management aren't soft skills. They're the difference between a rollout that actually changes how the business runs and one that quietly gets ignored until everyone goes back to the old way.

At Scale, Gut Feelings Stop Working

At Microsoft's scale, nobody's intuition is reliable enough to bet a budget on. The A/B testing program I built was based on a simple premise: stop assuming you know what works and start finding out. That's shaped how I approach every marketing decision since. Opinions are cheap. Data tells you what to do next.
